Prompt patterns — a working catalogue

Eight patterns the major model labs converge on, each shown as a side-by-side weak prompt → stronger prompt with the kind of output you should expect. Patterns 1–5 deepen the five from Module 1's Prompt patterns that work; patterns 6–8 add techniques that didn't fit in the module body but are worth knowing.

1. Set the role and the audience

Anthropic's term for this is "give Claude a role". The model behaves measurably differently depending on who it thinks it is talking to and as. State both, up front.

Weak

Write me a memo about the new data classification policy.

Stronger

You are a senior policy analyst at the Ministry of
Communications. Your reader is a director who has 5
minutes before a meeting. Write a one-page memo on the
new data classification policy: what changes, who is
affected, what they need to do this week.

The stronger prompt swapped three words of vague intent for five sentences of role, audience, length, and outcome. The model's first draft is now usable instead of generic.

2. Show, don't tell — multishot examples

Anthropic explicitly recommends "using examples effectively" and notes that examples are "denser than rules." A model with one good example will usually outperform a model with three pages of style guidelines.

Worked example — matching house style

Here are two acknowledgement letters in our standard
house style:

<example>
Dear citizen,
Thank you for your enquiry of 12 May. We have logged
your case as #4471 and a specialist will respond within
five working days. You can check status at sa.gov.sa/4471.
Sincerely,
Customer Service Team
</example>

<example>
Dear Mr. Al-Otaibi,
We received your appeal of 08 May. Reference #4499. A
review officer is assigned and will write within ten
working days.
Sincerely,
Appeals Office
</example>

Now write the acknowledgement letter for this new case:
<case>
Subject: complaint about delayed visa processing.
Citizen name: Sarah Mohammed. Date received: 14 May.
Case number: 4523. Expected response window: 7 working days.
Department: Visa Services.
</case>

Two example letters teach the model the tone, the level of formality, the case-number placement, and the closing. Without those examples the model would invent its own house style and you'd spend ten exchanges correcting it.

3. Ask for the structure first

Cheap to course-correct an outline. Expensive to course-correct three pages of prose. The pattern: stop the model before it generates the long thing, agree on the skeleton, then let it expand.

Weak

Write a 4-page briefing on adopting MCP across our agency.

Stronger

Don't write the briefing yet. First propose an outline:
4 sections, 1 sentence per section explaining what it
will cover, ordered for a 15-minute reading time. I'll
tell you which sections to expand and which to drop.

4. Push back when you disagree

These tools are extraordinarily responsive to feedback. The sooner you treat them like a junior colleague who needs direction (rather than a vending machine that produces a single output), the more useful they become. Be specific about which sentence is wrong and why.

Weak

This isn't quite right. Try again.

Stronger

Your second paragraph assumes the reader knows what
the data classification standard is. They don't.
Rewrite that paragraph as if explaining to a director
who has never read the standard. Keep paragraphs 1
and 3.

5. Catch confident wrongness

The single biggest failure mode of today's tools is producing fluent, confident text that is plausible-sounding but factually wrong — hallucination. Two prompting moves materially reduce it:

  1. Give the model the document and ask it to cite. “Here is the policy PDF. Quote the exact sentence that supports each claim in your summary.”
  2. Give it permission to say it doesn't know. “If a question can't be answered from the attached document, say so explicitly — do not guess.”

Both of these add what Anthropic calls "context to improve performance" — the model has a ground truth to anchor against, and a permitted exit if it doesn't.

6. Let it think first — chain of thought

For any prompt that involves reasoning — comparing options, finding errors, doing math, evaluating a trade-off — ask the model to think before answering. Anthropic phrases this as “letting Claude think” in their prompting best practices; it is the same idea as the chain-of-thought technique that put GPT-style reasoning on the map in 2022.

Weak

Which vendor (A, B, or C) gives us the best value?

Stronger

Compare vendors A, B, and C on five dimensions:
price, sovereignty, integration cost, vendor lock-in,
and 24-month roadmap. Think through each dimension
out loud before recommending a vendor. Show your
reasoning for each dimension, then give the
recommendation in the last paragraph.

This isn't a prompt-engineering trick — it is how the model produces better answers. Forcing it to lay out the steps reduces the failure mode where the first token committed to an answer locks in a wrong one.

7. Structure inputs with tags

When a prompt mixes instructions, context, and data, separate them with XML-style tags. Anthropic's models are explicitly trained to recognise this convention and respond more accurately when it's used.

Worked example — tagged for clarity

You are reviewing draft government correspondence.

<policy>
Letters must address the recipient by their full name
on first mention, then by family name only thereafter.
Avoid hedging phrases ("we think", "perhaps").
</policy>

<letter>
[paste the draft letter here]
</letter>

Review the letter against the policy. Quote each
sentence that violates a rule and suggest a fix.
Output format:
<findings>
<issue>...</issue>
</findings>

The model now knows precisely which block is the rule, which is the data, and how the answer should look. This pattern scales: when prompts grow, structure prevents them from collapsing.

8. Chain prompts when one is too much

If a single prompt has to do three things, do them in three prompts. Anthropic's "chain complex prompts" guidance and the prompt-chaining workflow pattern from our agent patterns page are the same idea at different scale: split the work, gate between steps.

The leader's heuristic

If you find yourself adding a fifth instruction to a prompt that already has four, stop and ask: could the first three be a separate prompt whose output feeds the next? Almost always, yes — and the chained version is more reliable, easier to debug, and cheaper if you can use a smaller model for the easy steps.
